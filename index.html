04The portfolio — assets to build

Hiring managers in writing / research / content / PMM roles hire on artifacts, not credentials. They want process, not pixels — which means Figma polish matters far less than you fear. Build these in order; each is a weekend-sized deliverable. Click any asset to expand the step-by-step.

1Positioning one-liner & retitled identity~1 hr›

Before any artifact, fix how you're labeled — recruiters for adjacent roles search by keyword, and "UX designer" filters you toward the Figma jobs you don't want.

  1. Pick a hybrid title you'll lead with everywhere (LinkedIn headline, CV, portfolio hero): "UX Writer & Content Designer" or "UX Researcher / Content Strategist." Match it to whichever top-3 bet you're prioritizing.
  2. Write one positioning sentence. Template: "I turn messy product problems into clear words and tested decisions — including with AI tools that let me prototype faster than most." Edit it to sound like you.
  3. List your 3 proof points in a sentence each — the evidence you'll back the claim with (a teardown, a study, an AI build). These become your case studies below.
  4. Stress-test it: read 5 real target job posts, and make sure your one-liner uses words that appear in them (content, messaging, research, strategy, AI).
✦ AI assist
Paste 5 job descriptions into an LLM and ask it to "extract the 15 most common skills and keywords, then draft 3 headline options and a positioning sentence that use them." Generate 10 variations, then pick and edit — AI is faster at divergent options than you are at a blank page.
Output: one headline + one positioning sentence + three proof points, saved in a doc you'll reuse on every profile.
2A 1-page portfolio sitehalf day›

The home that everything links to. Zero design skill required — content carries it. (This very site can be that home.)

  1. Pick the easiest tool: a public Notion page (free, fastest), Framer, or Read.cv. Don't shop tools for more than 20 minutes.
  2. Use this structure, top to bottom: hero line (your one-liner) → 3 case-study cards → short "about" → contact (email + LinkedIn).
  3. Write each case-study card as a teaser: title, one-line problem, the outcome, a thumbnail. Each links to the full case study.
  4. Add an "AI toolkit" strip naming the specific tools you use (LLM, no-code builder, research tool) — this is your differentiator, make it visible.
  5. Ship it half-finished. Publish with one case study live and "2 more coming" — a live URL beats a perfect draft.
✦ AI assist
Use a no-code/AI site builder (Lovable, v0, Framer AI) — describe the page and let it generate the layout, then drop your content in. Ask an LLM to "write a warm 80-word About section from these facts" and to proofread every page. Building this with AI is itself a talking point.
Output: one public URL you can paste into every application.
Tools: Notion / Framer / Read.cv
3aCase study — UX writing / content design1 weekend›

The fastest credible artifact, and it needs only a doc and screenshots — no Figma.

  1. Pick a real app you use that has weak copy (banking, fitness, a SaaS trial). Real product = real stakes in the story.
  2. Choose one flow to fix: onboarding, an empty state, error messages, a paywall, or a confirmation. One flow, done deeply.
  3. Screenshot the "before" and annotate what's wrong — vague, jargon, no next step, wrong tone. This is the research.
  4. Rewrite the copy as a before/after table. State the principle behind each change (clarity, voice, reduce anxiety, drive action).
  5. Show it in context: paste your new copy over the screenshots (Google Slides or FigJam — drag text onto the image, no design skill).
  6. Write the narrative: Context → Problem → What I did → Outcome/learning → Tools. ~400 words.
✦ AI assist
Feed the screenshots/copy to a multimodal LLM and ask it to "critique this UX copy and list every clarity, tone, and next-step problem." Then have it generate 3 rewrite options per string so you can choose — and ask it to "name the UX-writing principle behind each change" to sharpen your narrative. You make the final calls; AI accelerates the audit and the drafting.
Output: a before/after content case study proving you think in systems, not just sentences.
Tools: Google Docs/Slides · screenshots · FigJam (free)
3bCase study — UX research1–2 weekends›

Your direct-training artifact. One small but real study beats a theoretical write-up.

  1. Write one research question — e.g. "Where do first-time users of [app] get stuck in onboarding?" Narrow beats broad.
  2. Recruit 5 participants. Friends-of-friends count. Five is the recognized number for usability findings.
  3. Run the sessions: a 20-min moderated test over Google Meet (share screen, give tasks, stay quiet), or an unmoderated Maze test. Record with consent.
  4. Synthesize: dump observations onto sticky notes in FigJam, cluster into themes (affinity map), pull out the top 3 findings.
  5. Turn findings into recommendations — each finding paired with a concrete fix and a rough severity.
  6. Write it up with the affinity-map screenshot, the 3 findings, the recommendations, and what you'd do next with more time/budget.
✦ AI assist
Use AI to generate a discussion guide and task scenarios up front, auto-transcribe the sessions (Otter, or your meeting tool), then paste transcripts in and ask it to "cluster these observations into themes and surface the top 3 findings with supporting quotes." Verify every theme against the raw transcript yourself — AI speeds synthesis, but you own the findings.
Output: an end-to-end research story — question → method → synthesis → recommendation. Doubles as asset #5.
Tools: Google Meet/Forms · FigJam (free) · Maze (free tier)
3cCase study — your AI edge1 weekend›

The asset that separates you from every other junior applicant. You've already built the thing — you just haven't written the story.

  1. Pick one AI no-code project you've built and frame it as a product, not a demo.
  2. Answer the product questions: What problem? For whom? What did you build? What tools/prompts powered it? What worked, what didn't?
  3. Add a "what I'd test next" section — name the hypothesis and how you'd validate it. This shows product judgment, not just building.
  4. Document the AI workflow explicitly: the prompting approach, the no-code stack, any iterations. Hiring managers want to see you can ship with AI.
  5. Include a screenshot or short Loom of it working. Proof it's real.
✦ AI assist
This case study is the AI story — make the workflow the star. Show the actual prompts you wrote, the no-code stack, and the iterations. Ask an LLM to "interview me about this project and turn my answers into a structured case study" — answering its questions out loud is the fastest way to write the narrative.
Output: a "I ship with AI" case study — your single strongest differentiator across all three top-3 bets.
Tools: your existing build · Loom · a screenshot
4Messaging sample pack — for PMM / contenthalf day›

One page that proves you can do marketing-adjacent work. Content and PMM recruiters want exactly this.

  1. Pick a product you love (or invent a plausible one) and pretend you're launching it.
  2. Write a positioning statement — "For [audience] who [need], [product] is the [category] that [benefit], unlike [alternative]."
  3. Draft 3 value props, each as a benefit-led headline + one supporting line.
  4. Write the landing-page hero: headline, subhead, CTA button text.
  5. Add 2 social posts + 1 launch email — same message, adapted to each channel's voice.
✦ AI assist
Have an LLM draft the positioning statement and 5 value-prop angles, then you choose and sharpen. Ask it to "adapt this core message into a LinkedIn post, an X post, and a launch email, each in the right voice and length." Use an image AI (Canva, Midjourney) for a quick hero mockup. Editing AI drafts is exactly the modern content workflow these roles hire for.
Output: a one-page launch pack showing range from strategy (positioning) to execution (copy).
Tools: Google Docs · optional Canva for the hero mockup
5Insights deck — for research / CX / strategy2–3 hrs›

Mostly a repackage of asset 3b into the format insights and strategy teams actually present in.

  1. Slide 1 — the question and why it mattered (the business context).
  2. Slide 2 — method & who you talked to (participants, approach, in one tidy slide).
  3. Slides 3–5 — the 3 findings, one per slide, each with a supporting quote or data point.
  4. Final slide — the recommendation and projected impact. End on the "so what."
  5. Design it plainly: a clean template, lots of white space. Clarity reads as senior; decoration doesn't.
✦ AI assist
Paste your findings and ask an LLM to "structure these into a 6-slide insights narrative with a one-line takeaway per slide." Use an AI deck tool (Gamma, Pitch, or Google Slides' Gemini) to generate the formatted slides from your outline so you spend time on the story, not the layout.
Output: a 6-slide deck proving you can turn raw research into a decision a team can act on.
Tools: Google Slides / Pitch · reuse 3b
6Tuned CV + LinkedInhalf day›

The distribution layer. The best portfolio fails if the CV gets filtered out first.

  1. Mirror the keywords of your target roles — pull the recurring terms from 10 real job posts and make sure they appear naturally in your CV and LinkedIn.
  2. Lead each role/project with an outcome, not a duty. "Ran a 5-user study that surfaced 3 onboarding fixes" beats "responsible for research."
  3. Make AI fluency a named skill block — list the specific tools (LLM, no-code builder, research tool), not a vague "AI" footnote.
  4. Set the LinkedIn headline to your hybrid title from asset #1, and add the portfolio URL to the featured/contact section.
  5. Turn on "Open to work" for your target titles and the Tel Aviv + remote regions.
✦ AI assist
Paste a target job description plus your CV and ask an LLM to "rewrite my bullets to mirror this role's keywords and lead each with an outcome" — then make a tailored version per application. Have it "draft a LinkedIn headline and About section in the first person" and run an ATS-keyword gap check against each posting. Always edit for truth and your own voice.
Output: a keyword-tuned CV + LinkedIn that point straight at your portfolio — recruiters find people this way.
Tools: your portfolio URL · 10 target job posts

How to brainstorm work when you have no client projects

The classic junior trap is "I have nothing to show." Here's how to generate legitimate material from scratch — click any tactic for how to run it.

Expand allCollapse all

ATeardowns / redesignsunlimited supply›

The highest-leverage source: pick a product with a visible flaw and fix it. No client, no permission needed, and it shows judgment.

  1. Keep a running list of moments a product annoyed or confused you — that's your backlog.
  2. Pick one and scope it tight: a single flow or screen, not the whole app.
  3. Diagnose before you fix — write why it fails (unclear, no next step, wrong tone) so the work reads as thinking, not opinion.
  4. Publish the before/after with the principle behind each change. This is case study 3a.
Why it works: demonstrates the exact judgment hiring managers screen for, with zero dependency on anyone else.
BSpeculative / concept projectslabel them clearly›

Invent a brief and execute it. Fully accepted for juniors — as long as the process is real and you label it as a concept.

  1. Write yourself a realistic brief: a fake company, a real problem, constraints.
  2. Do the actual work — research, drafts, iterations — and document the messy middle, not just the polished end.
  3. Mark it "concept project" openly. Honesty reads as confidence; pretending it was real reads as risk.
Why it works: lets you target a specific role's exact deliverable when no real project exists yet.
CVolunteer / nonprofit workreal stakeholder›

A real client, even an unpaid one, turns a teardown into a story with stakes and constraints.

  1. Find a small org — a local NGO, a charity, a friend's early startup, or a platform like Catchafire.
  2. Offer one scoped deliverable: rewrite their onboarding emails, audit their site copy, run a small user study. Not "help with everything."
  3. Capture the real constraints and feedback — stakeholder pushback is exactly what makes the case study credible.
Why it works: "real client" outranks "concept" in an interview, and you get a reference.
D"Rewrite of the week" seriesportfolio + reach›

Post one short microcopy or research teardown every week on LinkedIn. It builds the portfolio and the visibility at the same time — recruiters find people this way.

  1. Commit publicly to a weekly cadence and a fixed day (this is also the forcing function in section 06).
  2. Keep each post tiny: one before/after, one principle, one image. 15 minutes, not a weekend.
  3. Tag the pattern, not the company — critique the design choice, stay professional about the brand.
  4. Collect the best 3–4 into a portfolio case study at month's end.
Why it works: compounding — each post is an artifact and a reach event that puts you in recruiters' feeds.
EReframe your AI buildsalready done›

You don't need new work here — each AI no-code project you've shipped already is a case study. You just haven't written the narrative around it.

  1. List every AI/no-code thing you've built, however small.
  2. Reframe each as content / conversation / research, not "design" — this neutralizes the Figma gap and lands where demand is.
  3. Apply the asset 3c structure (problem → user → build → what's next) to your strongest one.
Why it works: turns work you've already done into your single biggest differentiator, for free.
FDepth over breadththe rule›

The meta-rule that governs all of the above.

  1. Aim for 3 strong case studies, not 8 thin ones — depth signals seniority; a long list of shallow pieces signals the opposite.
  2. Show the messy middle in each — the dead ends and the changes you made are what prove you can think.
  3. Cut anything you can't speak to confidently for 5 minutes in an interview.
Why it works: interviewers probe depth; a few deep stories survive scrutiny that a wide-but-shallow portfolio won't.

06The forcing function: a 4-week "Ship in Public" sprint

Motivation evaporates without an external deadline. Announce on LinkedIn: "For the next 4 weeks I'm publishing one UX writing/research teardown every Friday." Public commitment + weekly cadence = built-in accountability and recruiter visibility at once.

Week 1
Content teardown. Audit a real app's copy, publish the before/after redesign. → Case study 3a.
Week 2
AI-build case study. Write the narrative around one of your no-code projects. → Case study 3c, your differentiator.
Week 3
Research study. Run the 5-person study, publish the insights deck. → Case study 3b + 5.
Week 4
Messaging pack. Mock launch for a product you love. → Asset 4, aimed at content/PMM roles.
Two more forcing functions to stack: (1) Apply backwards from job descriptions — pull 10 real TLV posts, extract every required tool, build the portfolio piece that proves each. The market becomes your curriculum. (2) Weekly review — book a recurring 20-min slot with someone who gets a deliverable every Friday. External eyes = accountability.

End state after 4 weeks: a full 3-case-study portfolio, 4 posts of recruiter reach, the Tier-1 skills named on your CV, and an income stream running. Then point applications at the top-3 bets.
